Speaker Notes

That convergence you just saw — there's serious engineering behind it. Let me show you what's actually happening. When we see 'Modeling' on a resume, we don't guess. We score four independent signals — skill embedding similarity, the person's job title, their company context, and what other skills they have — each weighted by reliability. For a Financial Analyst at Goldman Sachs whose other skills include Bloomberg Terminal and DCF analysis — that resolves to Financial Modeling with high confidence. A 3D Artist at Pixar with Maya and ZBrush? 3D Modeling. The signals converge. But here's what really sets this apart: when a canonical skill has variants where the job title patterns genuinely disagree — like 'Automation' appearing on both QA engineers and marketing managers — we detect that statistically. We cluster the title embeddings, check the separation, and if it's real, propose a split. A language model validates the split before anything changes in the taxonomy. This handles polysemy at scale, automatically. And the taxonomy heals itself. Agents continuously scan for mismatches and fix them. Near-duplicate canonicals get auto-merged. New skills always match against existing canonicals first — the system never creates redundancy. This is why it's defensible. You need the data, the algorithms, and years of feedback loops to build something like this.
