Speaker Notes

So that mess from the last slide — cleaning up one person's skills is actually the easy part. What we built is a living taxonomy — a knowledge graph that grows smarter with every skill it processes. New hire comes in with unusual skills on their resume? They auto-canonize against what's already there through our API, with job title context for disambiguation. No redundancy, no manual mapping. Here's the insight that makes this powerful: employees describe their skills in their own words — 'Python ML pipelines with Kubeflow.' But job profiles need standardized language — 'Machine Learning.' Our taxonomy links both through variant-to-canonical mappings. That's what makes real gap analysis possible — comparing a person's actual skills against a job's requirements even when they use completely different language. And because every canonical carries structured labels, you're not limited to skill-by-skill comparison. You can ask: what's our digital readiness gap? Our AI readiness? Our cloud coverage? Any dimension you care about. The engineering challenge behind this is massive — deduplicating across every employee and job profile in your organization, hundreds of thousands of skill mentions, while keeping the taxonomy company-scoped and versioned so you can track how your skill language evolves. That's what our canonizer was built to do.
