Why This Is Hard to Replicate
The pattern engine draws on extensive real-world data — it knows that "Manager II — DA" at one company maps to "Manager, Data Analytics" because it's observed the pattern across thousands of organizations.
The semantic parser handles novel titles and internal codes that have never appeared in training data — but the pattern engine validates and constrains those suggestions against the real-world distribution.
Standardization isn't just string normalization. It's semantic resolution — decomposing a title into seniority, function, and domain, then mapping it to a position in our ontology that connects to skills, proficiency, and career trajectories.

Speaker Notes

Let me show you something that looks simple but is actually one of the hardest problems in talent intelligence — title standardization. Every company invents its own naming conventions. "Sr. Mgr, Data and Analytics," "Head of BI," "Manager II — DA" — these are all variations of similar roles, but no two companies express them the same way. Here's how we solve it. First, we take the raw titles as input. Then we run them through our proprietary resolution engine. The pattern recognition layer — trained on millions of real profiles — catches the patterns it's seen before. The semantic parsing layer handles the novel cases — internal codes, creative naming, abbreviations it hasn't encountered. These two layers are fused, not competing. Pattern recognition brings data-driven precision; semantic parsing brings linguistic flexibility. Next, we refine using context — seniority, organizational function, industry. "Head of BI" in finance vs. tech means different things. Finally, every title resolves to a canonical form in our ontology, with seniority, function, and archetype attached. This is what flows into everything else — the skills taxonomy, the proficiency model, the career pathways, the search engine. If your title layer is messy, everything downstream breaks. Ours isn't.
