Speaker Notes

This is one of the most important things to understand about talent intelligence: skills don't stand still. What you're seeing is a real-time visualization of how talent attribute clusters have drifted over the past decade, computed across our dataset of millions of career histories. Start on the left in 2015 — you see two clearly separated clusters. Blue is marketing and analytics: Excel, campaign metrics, segmentation. Purple is data engineering: SQL, ETL pipelines, Hadoop. These two worlds barely touched. Now watch what happens as we move through time. By 2018, a few bridge skills start appearing — Python shows up in both clusters. By 2020, SQL has migrated from being a purely data engineering skill to appearing in marketing analytics profiles. By 2023, you see significant overlap. Skills like Python, SQL, data visualization, and basic statistics are now shared across both domains. The overlap has gone from zero to around 50 percent. Take two real candidates: Candidate A was a Marketing Analyst in 2015, Candidate B was a Data Engineer. Eight years ago, their skill profiles had nothing in common. Today, Candidate A's evolved skill set has a 50 percent overlap with Candidate B's current profile. This has massive implications. Static skill taxonomies decay — if you're still using a skill framework from five years ago, it's already wrong. Talent pools that seem unrelated today may converge tomorrow, and if you can detect that early, you unlock strategic hiring advantages. And career pathway recommendations must account for where the landscape is heading, not just where it is today. That's what our temporal intelligence layer captures.
