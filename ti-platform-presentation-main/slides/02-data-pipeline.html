Overview

From Raw Data to Structured Intelligence

Censia has spent nearly a decade building a structured entity graph of over 550M professional profiles, spanning people, skills, roles, companies, industries, and the relationships between them. It is sourced from thousands of data sources, then normalized, deduplicated, and continuously enriched with validated and AI-inferred attributes. Think of it as the professional world’s equivalent of Google’s Knowledge Graph—not keyword matching, but true contextual understanding. Censia knows far more than where someone worked; it understands their role, inferred skills, career trajectory, and how they connect to thousands of other entities across the labor market.

Speaker Notes

Let me walk you through how we actually build the intelligence layer. It starts with data — and not the clean kind. We're pulling from hundreds of sources: people data, company data, job demand signals, industry classifications, salary benchmarks. It comes in every format imaginable, full of inconsistencies and duplicates. The pipeline has five stages. First, we ingest everything — raw pulls from all these sources. Then we normalize and standardize — cleaning up encoding issues, format conflicts, and schema mismatches across providers. The third stage is where it gets interesting: group and merge. Our entity resolution algorithms compare entire work histories to figure out which records belong to the same person, the same company, the same role. This is the hardest part — we're de-duplicating and linking across 550 million profiles. Fourth, we score and label everything. Recency scoring, maturity scoring, seniority classification, organizational function labeling, and dozens of other derived attributes. We also compute dense vector representations across multiple dimensions for downstream intelligence. Finally, we index and embed. Sparse embedding models are applied during the indexing pipeline to produce the final structured, searchable output. The transformation is dramatic: we go from what I call "data slop" — messy, inconsistent, duplicated — to clean, structured, scored intelligence — a Golden Record for every professional.
